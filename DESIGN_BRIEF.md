## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

This is the single source of truth for every image that appears on the Tiesverse website and across the Tiesverse ecosystem. It has two halves:

1. **The Worldbuilding & System half (Sections 1–14).** This explains *what Tiesverse is*, *why it looks the way it looks*, and the fixed rules every image must obey — philosophy, the five symbols, architecture, materials, colour, light, atmosphere, sky, humans, vegetation, water, rendering style, composition and emotion. Read this first. If you only memorise one thing, memorise Section 1 and the Final One-Line Test in Section 22.

2. **The Specification half (Sections 15–22).** This is the practical part: technical delivery standards, then a **master table of every placeholder still open on the site**, then a **detailed brief for each individual placeholder** — exact pixel dimensions, aspect ratio, the filename to deliver against, whether it sits on a light or dark surface, what it must depict, which symbol it maps to, the colour emphasis, the mood, composition notes, explicit do/don'ts, and a priority rating.

A designer should be able to read the first half and understand the universe, then read the relevant row in the second half and produce a finished, drop-in asset without asking a single follow-up question. Where a filename is given (e.g. `brand-establishing.png`), deliver the asset under that exact name so it can be placed into the build with no code change.

A note on scope: the website's interface colour (the saffron used on buttons) is a separate, deliberately restrained UI accent and is **not** the subject of this brief. This brief governs **imagery** — the illustrated and photographed world. The two share a warm family but are specified independently. Do not try to match the artwork to a hex used on a button; match the artwork to the palette in Section 5.

# PART ONE — THE WORLD

## 1. CORE PHILOSOPHY

Tiesverse is **not**:

- a media company
- a news company
- a temple
- a mythology project
- a fantasy civilization
- a historical recreation
- a startup
- a software brand

Tiesverse **is**:

> **A civilizational intelligence system.**

Every image should feel like one of these five acts: **observation, memory, transmission, foresight, connection.** Never like worship, conquest, fantasy, empire or spectacle.

Most institutions borrow their legitimacy from the present. Governments borrow it from authority. Companies borrow it from growth. Media companies borrow it from attention. Universities borrow it from credentials. **Tiesverse borrows its legitimacy from civilization itself.** When somebody encounters a Tiesverse image, they should not feel they are looking at a startup, a media platform or a website. They should feel they are looking at something that belongs to a long historical continuum — something that existed before them and will exist after them.

The visual system therefore communicates **permanence rather than novelty, depth rather than trend, memory rather than marketing, observation rather than performance.** Every creative decision is a vote for one side of those pairs. Always choose permanence, depth, memory and observation.

This is the test that sits above every other rule in this document, and it is repeated at the end because it matters more than any single specification:

> **Does this look like the visual identity of a civilizational intelligence institution that has existed for 500 years and will exist for 500 more?**

If the answer is not immediately *yes*, the image is wrong and must be revised.

## 2. THE CENTRAL IDEA — INSTITUTION, NOT MONUMENT

Tiesverse is not a city, a kingdom, an empire or a religion. It is **a network of institutions.** This single distinction governs more decisions than any colour or material rule:

- **Monuments celebrate. Institutions observe.**
- **Monuments remember the past. Institutions help society understand reality.**

When you are tempted to make something grand, ask whether you are making it grand the way a *monument* is grand (to be admired, to commemorate, to glorify a ruler or a god) or the way an *institution* is grand (because the work it does is vast). Always aim for the second. A Tiesverse observatory is not impressive because someone important built it; it is impressive because it is large enough to read the entire sky. The grandeur is functional, never ceremonial.

## 3. THE PARALLEL-INDIA PREMISE

This is the imaginative engine of the whole system, and it is worth holding in your mind constantly as you work.

Most fictional civilizations are imagined through **power** — kings, armies, palaces, conquest. Tiesverse must be imagined through **understanding** — surveying, recording, measuring, observing, archiving, transmitting, forecasting.

So imagine a parallel India. Not ancient India, not future India, not fantasy India. **A parallel India in which civilization invested its greatest resources not in kings and dynasties, but in systems for understanding the world.** In that India:

- observatories expanded into cities,
- archives expanded into institutions,
- river systems expanded into information networks,
- navigation systems expanded into geopolitical intelligence.

That parallel India is the world you are drawing. It is why a Jantar Mantar matters more than a palace, why a river observatory matters more than a throne, and why a survey tower matters more than a fort. The infrastructure of knowledge replaces the infrastructure of power as the subject of every image.

## 4. THE FIVE PERMANENT SYMBOLS

There are exactly five symbols in the Tiesverse universe. They are **functional, never decorative.** Each maps a structure to a meaning to a part of the organisation. Memorise this table; it recurs throughout the placeholder briefs.

| Sign | Meaning | Maps to (function) | Never depict as |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Observatory** | Observation | Research | A temple, a figure at prayer, a worshipper |
| **River** | Transmission | Media | A camera, a microphone, a film reel |
| **Archive** | Memory | Knowledge | Decorative manuscripts worn as costume / antiquity |
| **Tower** | Foresight | The future | A fortress, a watchtower, a weapon |
| **Constellation** | Connection | Network | A glowing-node tech "network", servers, circuitry |

Deep notes on each symbol, because designers consistently get these subtly wrong:

**The Observatory = research, intelligence, forecasting, pattern recognition.** It is the instrument of the patient mind: stone shaped precisely enough to hold the sky still. It asks the first question every researcher asks — *what is actually happening, beneath what we are told?* Whenever research must be represented, the observatory appears, sometimes explicitly, sometimes as a silhouette on the horizon. The reference object is Jantar Mantar and the Ujjain observatory tradition: monumental masonry instruments, ramps, gnomons, graduated arcs, calibration markings. It is a machine made of stone. It must never read as a temple. The structure is the character — not any human worshipping at it.

**The River = media, distribution, the spread of an idea.** This is the most important recurring motif after the observatory. The river is not in the images because India has rivers; it is there because rivers are *information systems*. Historically, people, goods, ideas and cultures all moved along rivers. In Tiesverse, **information moves along rivers.** Along the banks rise cities, ports and towers — the proof that understanding became reach. When you draw the river, you are drawing media: transmission, scale, the journey of knowledge from the observatory out into the world. Never represent media with a camera, a microphone or a film reel. Show the flow, not the equipment.

**The Archive = memory.** Not a library — *memory*. Maps, measurements, charts: the record a civilization keeps so the next mind begins where the last one ended. An archive should feel **sacred without being religious** — and this distinction is delicate and essential. The reverence comes from the value of knowledge, never from worship. Think carved stone halls of records, courtyards of charts, terraces of measurement — working memory, not a museum of antiquity.

**The Tower = foresight.** The tower sees farther than everyone else; that is the whole of its meaning. It must **never** look defensive, never resemble a military watchtower, never read as a fort. It is a civilizational instrument for looking ahead — the habit of seeing the next thing before everyone else does, and naming it. Where forecasting, strategy or "the view over the horizon" must be shown, the tower appears.

**The Constellation = connection, networks, the lines between events.** Stars are only points until a mind draws the lines between them. The constellation is the act of meaning itself — joining an election here to a war there, a market to a monsoon, until the scattered world reads as one story. It must **never** become a generic tech "network" of glowing nodes. These are *stars*, not servers; *drawn lines of understanding*, not data cables.

## 5. THE ARCHITECTURAL DOCTRINE

Architecture in Tiesverse is **never decoration — it is narrative.** Every structure must have a reason to exist, and the viewer should subconsciously understand *"I know what this structure does"* without explanation. Architecture must communicate **function before beauty.**

**Allowed — Indian civilizational infrastructure:** Jantar Mantar; the Ujjain observatory; stepwells (baolis); ghats and riverfronts; survey platforms and measurement instruments; stone observatories; archives and knowledge courtyards; navigation infrastructure and cartographic systems; river-monitoring systems; rock-cut architecture (Ellora, the Kailasa temple stone language, cave architecture); carved stone halls; chhatris; pillars; stone bridges; stone terraces; knowledge plazas.

**Forbidden — absolutely avoid:** Arabic, Persian and Ottoman architecture; Dune aesthetics; Mesopotamian or Egyptian fantasy; generic fantasy-civilization design; minaret skylines; palace cities; fortress cities; castles; domes-everywhere; Sultanate visual language; Mughal-heavy silhouettes as the primary language.

**The hard filter:** if somebody could mistake the image for *Dune*, *Aladdin*, *Arabian Nights* or a *Persian Empire*, it is wrong. Discard it and start again.

### 5.1 The Stone Language — carved, not built

One of the strongest discoveries from our references is that Indian civilizational architecture feels **carved rather than assembled.** European architecture often feels *built*. Indian civilizational architecture often feels *excavated — revealed — extracted from the landscape.* This feeling is essential and it is the single fastest way to make an image feel authentically Tiesverse rather than generic-ancient.

So the instruction to every designer is: imagine *"what if the mountain itself became architecture?"* — never *"what if architecture was placed on the mountain?"* Ellora's Kailasa is the supreme reference: it was not built, it was **released** from the rock. Future Tiesverse architecture follows this principle. Structures should look as though they were cut down into living stone and revealed, with the geology continuing seamlessly into the masonry.

### 5.2 The Landscape is a participant, not a background

Most modern visual systems isolate architecture from nature. **Tiesverse integrates them.** Observatories emerge from cliffs. Archives emerge from stone terraces. Survey towers rise out of river valleys. Stepwells descend into the geology. Everything should feel **rooted; nothing should feel placed.** If you can imagine lifting the structure off the ground and setting it somewhere else, it is wrong — it must belong to that specific piece of earth.

## 6. MATERIAL LANGUAGE

Everything should feel **carved, not constructed.**

| Material | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Sandstone | Primary |
| Terracotta stone | Primary |
| Weathered granite | Primary |
| Carved rock | Primary |
| Stone steps | Primary |
| Survey stone (graduated, marked masonry) | Primary |

**Avoid entirely:** shiny marble, chrome, polished gold, sci-fi metal, glass skyscrapers. Surfaces are matte, weathered, sun-worn and tactile. Edges are softened by time and dust. Nothing gleams; nothing is mirror-finished. Where metal instruments appear (a gnomon, a graduated arc), they are aged bronze or worn brass at most — never bright, never reflective, never "tech".

## 7. COLOUR SYSTEM (FOR ARTWORK)

The orange-terracotta palette is **not branding — it is geography.** It comes from sandstone, terracotta, dust, sunset, carved rock and river sediment. The world should feel **born from stone**, not painted orange afterward. The palette must emerge naturally from the environment in the frame.

### Primary palette

| Colour | Hex | Where it lives |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Deep Terracotta | `#A54E2A` | Stone in shadow, foreground mass, carved depth |
| Burnt Saffron | `#D76A1E` | The dominant mid-tone; sunlit stone, the overall cast |
| Desert Orange | `#E98B47` | Light-struck planes, the warm side of forms |
| Warm Dust | `#D4B18B` | Haze, distant planes, the dissolving horizon |
| Stone Beige | `#C7A27D` | Pale stone, sky near the horizon, sand |

### Accent palette — use rarely, as a whisper

| Colour | Hex (guide) | Permitted use only |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Muted Indigo | `#3B4A63` | Night-observatory scenes, deep sky |
| Dusty Crimson | `#8C3A2E` | Civic banners, a single human marker |
| Faded Pink Blossom | `#D8A6A0` | Flowering trees, far landscape softening |
| River Blue | `#6E8CA0` | Water, and water only — always desaturated, never bright |

### Forbidden colours

Neon blue; neon green; cyberpunk purple; electric pink; saturated gold. **Why we especially avoid blue:** blue instantly reads as technology, cyberpunk, software, screens and future-tech, and that breaks the entire illusion. Tiesverse *is* technological, but it must never *look* technological — its technology is embedded inside civilization, not sitting on top of it. The only blue permitted is the muted, dusty River Blue, restricted to actual water, and even then kept low in saturation.

## 8. LIGHTING SYSTEM

Light carries the mood and the time-of-day discipline is strict.

- **Dawn — the best option.** First light raking low across stone; long soft shadows; the world waking.
- **Dusk — second best.** The warm collapse of the day; saffron deepening to terracotta.
- **Golden haze — third best.** Diffuse, particle-filled, the sun softened to a glow.

**Avoid:** harsh midday sun; hard direct sunlight; night-city lights; dramatic theatrical spotlighting. Light should feel *atmospheric and earned*, never staged. There is always a single, low, warm light source. Never two competing light sources; never a cold fill.

## 9. ATMOSPHERE & HAZE

Every image **must** contain haze, dust, depth and atmosphere. The horizon should disappear gradually; nothing should be perfectly sharp.

This is not an aesthetic preference — it is philosophical. Civilizations are larger than individuals. **Haze creates distance; distance creates scale; scale creates humility; humility creates wonder.** Atmospheric depth is therefore mandatory in every frame, not because it is cinematic, but because it communicates the true size of the world and the smallness of any single observer within it. Foregrounds may be relatively crisp; the middle distance softens; the far distance dissolves entirely into Warm Dust and Stone Beige.

## 10. SKY LANGUAGE

**The sky is the largest symbol in the entire system. The sky represents invisible intelligence.** The viewer should feel there is a system operating above everything — not divine, not magical, but *intellectual*.

**Allowed in the sky (subtle, discovered, never announced):** survey diagrams; coordinates; navigation systems; celestial calculations; cartographic markings; orbital geometry; measurement circles.

**Forbidden in the sky:** text; labels; random writing; glowing runes; fantasy symbols; astrological icons; zodiac graphics.

The single governing rule for sky graphics: they must feel **discovered, not designed.** A viewer should *find* them on a second look, not be hit by them on the first. The moment the sky markings become obvious, they have failed and must be dialed back until they are almost subliminal — faint as a watermark pressed into the air, the ghost of a measurement someone once made across the heavens.

## 11. HUMAN PRESENCE

Humans are **supporting characters, never heroes.** The protagonist of every image is the institution, the civilization, the system. Humans exist for exactly one reason: **to establish scale.** Nothing more.

Permitted human roles: observers, surveyors, researchers, travellers, navigators — small figures, usually seen from behind or in silhouette, dwarfed by the structure and the landscape. The benchmark image is *the single observer seated before the great observatory*: without the figure, it is beautiful architecture; with the figure, it becomes a civilization. That is the entire function of the human — to convert scale into meaning. Never a portrait-scale hero; never a triumphant pose; never a face that demands attention.

## 12. VEGETATION SYSTEM

**Preferred:** palms; flowering trees; banyan silhouettes; river vegetation; stepwell vegetation. **Avoid:** European forests; pine forests; fantasy mushrooms; jungle overload. Vegetation is sparse, Indian, warm-climate, and always subordinate to stone and sky. It frames and softens; it never takes over the frame.

## 13. WATER SYSTEM

**Water equals transmission.** Use rivers, canals, ghats, reservoirs and stepwells. Water must feel like **civilization moving**, not like *nature*. It is engineered, channelled, monitored, stepped — the visible circulatory system of the knowledge-civilization. Keep it desaturated (River Blue at most), reflective only of the warm sky, never a bright tropical blue.

## 14. ARTISTIC STYLE, COMPOSITION & EMOTION

**Rendering target:** roughly **70% matte painting, 20% oil painting, 10% photography.** It should feel hand-made and painterly, with the weight of a museum landscape and the texture of pigment — not the cleanliness of a render.

**Avoid:** hyperreal CGI; game concept art; Unreal-Engine looks; Marvel-poster looks. If it looks like a frame from a AAA game or a superhero key art, it is wrong.

**Composition — the three-zone rule:** a **monumental foreground**, a **vast middle distance**, and an **endless, dissolving horizon.** Always build **depth, scale and silence.** The eye should travel from a large, grounded, carved foreground element, across a softening middle distance, to a horizon that simply fades away. Negative space — quiet sky, empty plaza, still water — is not wasted; it is the silence in which the institution sits.

**Emotional target — what every image must evoke (all High priority):** Curiosity, Wonder, Reflection, Intelligence, Continuity. The precise emotional question we want a viewer to ask is: *"How did this world become this sophisticated?"*

**Emotions to avoid, and why each is wrong:** Fear (wrong — this is not a threatening world); Worship (wrong — reverence here is for knowledge, never deity); Aggression (wrong — nothing conquers); Triumph (wrong — institutions don't gloat); Spectacle (wrong — we are not a show). The dominant feeling is **wonder**, not pride, not patriotism, not nostalgia.

### What makes an image feel WRONG

An image is wrong when it feels like: fantasy; mythology; religion; a kingdom; a palace; an empire; a game environment; a movie poster; a startup landing page; or AI art. Instead it must feel **documented, observed, discovered, remembered.**

### The benchmark image (the formula that already works)

The strongest Tiesverse image discovered so far is built from exactly these ingredients, and it is the reference every new world-image should be measured against:

- a massive Indian observatory,
- a river valley,
- terracotta atmosphere,
- subtle celestial geometry,
- an endless horizon,
- a single observer,
- atmospheric haze,
- **no** text, **no** fantasy architecture, **no** palace, **no** crowd.

# PART ONE-B — CRAFT DEEP DIVES

These sections translate the philosophy into hand-level craft decisions. They exist because the difference between an image that *belongs* in Tiesverse and one that merely *looks Indian* lives almost entirely in these details.

## 14A. REFERENCES & TOUCHSTONES

Designers should build a personal reference board before drawing a single frame. Use the touchstones below as the centre of gravity. These are *starting points* for understanding the world's logic, never images to copy literally.

**Architectural touchstones (study these closely):**

- **Jantar Mantar (Jaipur, Delhi, Ujjain).** The single most important reference in the entire system. These are not buildings; they are *instruments built at the scale of buildings* — the Samrat Yantra, the Jai Prakash Yantra, graduated arcs, vast ramps that are really the edge of a sundial. This is exactly what Tiesverse means by "technology embedded in civilization": the most advanced thing the culture could do, expressed in carved stone. Whenever you are unsure what a Tiesverse structure should be, ask "what would the Jantar Mantar version of this be?"
- **Ellora, especially the Kailasa temple (Cave 16).** The supreme example of *carved, not built*. Kailasa was excavated top-down from a single basalt cliff — released from the rock, not assembled on it. Study how the geology becomes architecture with no visible seam. This is the soul of the stone language (Section 5.1).
- **Stepwells (Chand Baori, Rani-ki-Vav, Adalaj).** Architecture that *descends* into the earth in ordered geometry — the perfect form for the Archive ("storage", "memory") and for the idea that knowledge has depth. The repeating stair-geometry is a gift to composition.
- **The ghats (Varanasi, Maheshwar, Ujjain).** The interface between civilization and the river — terraces, steps, platforms meeting moving water. The reference for the River-as-media motif and for how human life sits, small, against monumental stone and flowing water.
- **Konark and the survey/measurement tradition; observatories of Ujjain.** For instruments, gnomons, graduated stone, calibration.
- **Rock-cut and cave architecture (Ajanta, Badami, Mahabalipuram shore reliefs).** For carved halls, pillared interiors, and stone that holds memory.

**Rendering / painting touchstones:** classical matte painting and 19th-century orientalist *landscape* painting (for atmosphere and scale only, never for subject), Hudson-River-School-scale luminism translated to terracotta, and museum-grade oil landscape. The target texture is pigment and light, not pixels and polygons.

**Anti-references (if your image resembles any of these, it is wrong):** *Dune* (Villeneuve) — the most common failure mode; its monolith-in-desert language is seductive and forbidden. *Assassin's Creed* and AAA game environments — too clean, too "explorable", too lit. *Aladdin / Arabian Nights / Prince of Persia* — wrong civilization entirely. Generic Midjourney "ancient temple" and "fantasy India" output — palace-heavy, gold-heavy, deity-heavy. Marvel/blockbuster key art — too theatrical, too high-contrast, hero-centred. Cyberpunk / sci-fi concept art — the blue-glow trap.

## 14B. THE FIVE SYMBOLS — HOW TO ACTUALLY DRAW THEM

A fuller drawing guide for each symbol, in foreground/middle/far terms.

**Observatory (Research).** *Foreground:* a fragment of the instrument itself — a graduated arc, the edge of a great gnomon, calibration marks worn into sandstone. *Middle:* the full masonry instrument rising, ramps and stairs giving it scale, a single observer at its base. *Far:* the valley and dissolving horizon, the faint survey geometry in the sky aligning with the instrument as if it is *reading* those very coordinates. Mood: patient, exact, awake. Do: make it legibly a machine for measuring the sky. Don't: add a shrine, an idol, an altar, a figure praying, or any dome.

**River (Media).** *Foreground:* a ghat or stone landing where the river meets built civilization. *Middle:* the river itself, luminous, carrying light, threading between settlements, ports, survey-towers along its banks. *Far:* the river narrowing into haze, the sense that it goes on transmitting forever. Mood: movement, reach, life. Do: emphasise *flow* and the things that rise along the banks because of it. Don't: show a camera, microphone, film reel, broadcast tower, or any modern media device. The river *is* the media.

**Archive (Knowledge / Memory).** *Foreground:* steps descending (stepwell language) or a terrace of charts and measurement stones. *Middle:* a hall or courtyard of records — ordered, vast, kept. *Far:* more of it than you can see, implying a civilization's entire memory. Mood: sacred-but-not-religious; the hush of a place that holds everything known. Do: make the reverence come from *knowledge*. Don't: render it as a temple, or dress it in decorative manuscripts as costume.

**Tower (Foresight / Future).** *Foreground:* the base of the tower meeting rock. *Middle:* a single, slender, instrument-like tower — clearly for *seeing*, not defending. *Far:* the horizon it is watching, the next thing arriving. Mood: anticipation, the calm of seeing first. Do: make it a civilizational instrument. Don't: make it a fort, a battlement, a watchtower with arrow-slits, or anything martial.

**Constellation (Connection / Network).** Usually expressed *in the sky* across any of the above, or as the subject of the atlas plate (A10). Faint drawn lines joining points — an election to a war, a market to a monsoon — the act of meaning. Mood: quiet revelation, the world resolving into one story. Do: keep it stars-and-drawn-lines, discovered. Don't: ever let it become glowing nodes, data cables, or a "tech network" diagram.

## 14C. SURFACE, WEATHERING & TEXTURE

Stone in Tiesverse is *old and used*, never new. Build in:

- **Erosion and softening** of edges — corners rounded by centuries of wind and dust.
- **Sediment and dust accumulation** in horizontal recesses; the lower portions of structures slightly buried or silted.
- **Sun-bleaching** on south/west faces; deeper saturated terracotta in the shadowed recesses.
- **Tool-marks and carving grain** that prove it was cut by hand from living rock.
- **Lichen, mineral staining, water-marks** near the river and stepwells — restrained, warm-toned, never green-heavy.
- **Matte, light-absorbing surfaces.** No specular highlights, no gloss, no wet-look except the river surface itself (and that only reflects warm sky).

The viewer's eye should believe it could run a hand over the stone and feel grain, heat and dust. If a surface looks injection-moulded, plastic, or freshly poured, it has failed.

## 14D. CAMERA, LENS & COMPOSITION

- **Eye level:** generally low-to-human — we look *up* at the institution slightly, reinforcing scale, but never a dramatic worm's-eye hero shot. The benchmark observer sits, so the camera often sits with them.
- **Lens feel:** natural to slightly wide (think 35–50mm equivalent), never fisheye, never a compressed telephoto "poster" look. Perspective is calm and true.
- **Horizon:** usually low (sky-dominant, because the sky is the largest symbol) or central; the horizon line itself should *dissolve* rather than cut hard.
- **Focal hierarchy:** one clear foreground anchor (carved mass), a legible mid-distance subject (the structure), and an empty, hazed far distance. Avoid competing focal points; avoid clutter.
- **Negative space is structural.** Quiet sky, an empty plaza, still water — these are where the institution *breathes*. Roughly half the frame may be atmosphere and emptiness. Silence is part of the composition, not a gap to fill.
- **Stillness over drama.** No motion blur, no action, no dynamic diagonals shouting for attention. The drama is the *scale and the quiet*, not an event.

## 14E. RESPONSIVE CROPPING & SAFE AREAS (why this matters technically)

The website is responsive: the same image is shown at different crops on desktop and mobile. Full-bleed images (hero, CTA, establishing) are cropped *more aggressively and more centrally* on narrow screens, and a dark scrim is layered over them in code so overlaid text stays legible.

Practical consequences for the artist:

- **Keep the essential subject centred** and within the middle ~80% of the frame. Anything critical near an edge may vanish on mobile.
- For **text-overlaid** images, keep the overlay region (stated per slot — usually centre or lower third) low in detail and tonally deep, because cream/white text will sit on it.
- Expect a **40–55% darkening scrim** on hero/CTA backgrounds — deliver them a touch brighter and richer than final, knowing they will be pushed down in code.
- For **card images** (event covers, guest portraits, signs), the card crops tight to the stated ratio with rounded corners — compose to the ratio exactly and keep the subject off the very edges.

## 14F. IMAGERY & THE EDITORIAL VOICE

The pictures must agree with the words. Tiesverse writes in a measured, literary, declarative voice ("Understanding is a civilizational act"; "A new kind of organisation, built for Bharat"). The imagery is the visual equivalent of that voice: composed, unhurried, certain, more interested in continuity than in novelty. An image that feels hype, urgent, trendy or loud contradicts the writing as surely as a typo would. When in doubt, make the image quieter, older and more certain.

# PART TWO — SPECIFICATION

## 15. TECHNICAL DELIVERY STANDARDS (read before exporting anything)

These apply to **every** asset unless a specific row overrides them.

**Resolution.** Deliver at **2× the pixel dimensions listed** for each placeholder (the table lists the design/display size; export at twice that on the long edge for retina sharpness). Example: a 1200×750 slot should be delivered at 2400×1500. Full-bleed hero/background images should be delivered at a minimum of **2560px on the long edge**, ideally 3000–3840px.

**Aspect ratio is mandatory and must be exact.** The layout crops to the stated ratio. If you deliver a different ratio, the image will be cropped by the browser and you lose control of the composition. Compose *inside* the stated ratio. Keep the essential subject within a **center-weighted safe area** (assume the outer 8–10% on each edge may be trimmed on some screens).

**File format.**
- Photographic or richly painted images → **JPG**, quality 82–90, optimised (target under ~400–600 KB where possible; hero images may go higher but compress sensibly).
- Images needing transparency, flat graphic elements, or crisp edges → **PNG**.
- Match the **existing extension** named in each row (some slots are wired to `.png`, others `.jpg`). If you must change the format, flag it so the code reference can be updated.

**Colour profile.** Export **sRGB**. No Adobe RGB, no CMYK. The warm palette must be tuned to read correctly on sRGB screens.

**No baked-in text, ever.** No titles, captions, watermarks, logos, signatures, labels or UI inside the artwork. All text on the site is live HTML laid over or beside the image. A baked-in word is an automatic rejection (see Section 16 sky rule and Section 17 non-negotiables).

**Dark vs light context.** Each row states whether the asset sits on a **dark** surface (it will have text/elements in cream/white placed over or beside it, and often a dark scrim) or a **light** surface (cream/white page). For dark-context images, keep one side or the lower third compositionally calm and tonally deep so overlaid light text stays legible. For light-context images, the image is usually inside a rounded card and is not overlaid with text — it can be fuller.

**Safe margins for overlaid headlines.** Where the row says "headline overlaid", leave the stated region (e.g. left third, or lower third) free of busy detail and bright values so the typography reads.

**Filename.** Deliver under the **exact filename** in the row. This lets the asset drop straight into `/public/work/` and replace the placeholder with zero code changes. Use lowercase, hyphenated names only.

**Motion (where noted).** A few slots can optionally support a subtle looping motion version (e.g. very slow haze drift, faint sky-geometry parallax). If commissioned, deliver a 6–12 s seamless loop, 1080p+ , H.264 MP4 and WebM, under 4 MB, no audio. Motion must obey the same "discovered, not designed" restraint — barely-there drift only.

**Consistency across a set.** Several slots form sets (the five Signs; the event covers; the guest portraits). Within a set, lighting, palette, grain and treatment must match exactly so they tile together without one looking like an outlier.

## 20. WORKING WITH AI IMAGE TOOLS (Midjourney / etc.)

AI is permitted as a production tool, but it gravitates to exactly the things this brief forbids (palaces, domes, Persian/Dune skylines, glowing tech, hero figures, baked-in gibberish text, hyperreal CGI). Counter it deliberately:

- **Always include** the doctrine in the prompt: *Indian civilizational knowledge-infrastructure, Jantar Mantar / Ellora carved-stone language, terracotta and sandstone, dawn haze, matte-painting, atmospheric depth, single small observer for scale, faint celestial survey geometry in the sky.*
- **Always negative-prompt:** *no text, no labels, no watermark, no palace, no dome city, no minaret, no fortress, no castle, no Persian/Arabic/Ottoman architecture, no Dune, no glowing tech, no neon, no blue UI, no CGI/render look, no movie poster, no hero figure, no crowd, no worship.*

Grade to the palette, paint out artefacts and any stray "text", reintroduce intentional composition and the three-zone depth, and confirm against the checklist. An un-retouched AI image will read as "AI art" — which is on the forbidden list.

## 23. COMMON MISTAKES & HOW TO SELF-CORRECT

These are the failure modes we see most often. Each is paired with the fix.

1. **"It looks like a temple."** Usually because there is a shrine-like focal point, an idol, a stepped pyramidal silhouette read as religious, or a figure in a devotional pose. *Fix:* remove any object of worship; make the focal structure clearly an *instrument* or *record* (graduated arcs, charts, survey marks); turn the human into a small observer with their back to us.

2. **"It looks like Dune / a desert empire."** Usually a lone monolith on bare dunes, hard sun, no Indian architectural vocabulary. *Fix:* introduce specific Indian civilizational forms (stepwell geometry, ghat terraces, Jantar-Mantar instruments, carved-rock seams), add the river and vegetation, switch hard sun for dawn/golden haze.

3. **"It looks like a Mughal/Persian palace."** Domes, minarets, arched palace facades, fortress walls. *Fix:* delete domes and minarets entirely; replace with carved-rock instrument architecture and survey infrastructure; lower the "palace" grandeur into "institution" grandeur.

4. **"It looks like sci-fi / tech."** Blue light, glow, screens, clean metal, node-networks. *Fix:* remove all blue and glow; express any "technology" as carved stone geometry (A6); move the constellation lines into faint sky marks, not luminous cables.

5. **"It looks like AI art."** Over-smooth, plastic surfaces, melted detail, gibberish "text", hyperreal CGI sheen, symmetrical mush. *Fix:* hand-grade and repaint; add real carving grain, weathering and dust; paint out all stray text; reintroduce intentional three-zone composition; matte it down (70% matte painting target).

6. **"It looks like a movie poster."** High contrast, a hero figure, theatrical rim-light, dynamic diagonal drama. *Fix:* flatten the drama into stillness; shrink/relocate the human to scale-only; replace theatrical light with one low, soft, warm source.

7. **"The sky graphics are too obvious."** Bright constellations, visible zodiac, glowing runes, legible text. *Fix:* dial the sky markings down until they are almost subliminal — faint survey geometry the viewer *discovers*, never reads.

8. **"It feels new."** Crisp edges, clean stone, no haze, no wear. *Fix:* age everything — erosion, sediment, sun-bleach, atmosphere; dissolve the horizon; make it feel 500 years old and still running.

9. **"The colour looks like an orange filter."** A uniform orange wash laid over a neutral image. *Fix:* rebuild the warmth from *materials and light* — terracotta stone, dawn sun, dust — so the colour is in the world, not on top of it. Use the full palette (deep terracotta shadows through stone beige highlights), not a single orange.

10. **"The human is the subject."** A portrait-scale or posed figure. *Fix:* shrink them, turn them away, push them to an edge or the base of the structure; they are punctuation, not the sentence.

## 24. GLOSSARY (for designers outside the Indian context)

- **Jantar Mantar** — early-18th-century astronomical observatories (Jaipur, Delhi, Ujjain, etc.) built as monumental masonry *instruments* for measuring time and tracking celestial bodies. The defining reference for Tiesverse "technology in stone".
- **Gnomon** — the part of a sundial/instrument that casts the shadow; at Jantar Mantar, a vast triangular stone ramp.
- **Samrat Yantra** — the "supreme instrument", a giant equinoctial sundial; archetypal Tiesverse object.
- **Ellora / Kailasa (Cave 16)** — a temple complex *carved downward out of a single rock cliff*; the reference for "carved, not built".
- **Baoli / Vav (stepwell)** — a well reached by descending ordered flights of stone steps; reference for the Archive and for "knowledge has depth".
- **Ghat** — stepped stone embankment where a town meets a river; reference for the River-as-media interface.
- **Chhatri** — an elevated, dome-shaped pavilion (umbrella); usable as a small civilizational marker (note: the *small* chhatri form is allowed; "domes everywhere" as a skyline is not).
- **Terracotta** — warm orange-brown fired-earth / stone tone; the dominant material and colour family.
- **Sandstone / weathered granite** — the primary structural stones; matte, warm, carveable.
- **Matte painting** — a painterly, non-photographic rendering technique used for environments and backgrounds; the dominant target style (70%).
- **Scrim** — a semi-transparent dark overlay applied in code over a background image so overlaid text stays legible; relevant to all full-bleed slots.
- **Safe area** — the central region of an image guaranteed to survive responsive cropping; keep the essential subject inside it.

## 25. CLOSING NOTE TO THE DESIGNER

You are not decorating a website. You are drawing the surface of a civilization that chose to understand the world rather than rule it. Every observatory you carve, every river you set moving, every horizon you let dissolve into dust is a small argument for the same idea: that knowledge, patiently built and faithfully kept, is the most monumental thing a people can make. Hold that idea, obey the palette and the doctrine, keep the human small and the sky vast — and the work will belong, unmistakably, to Tiesverse.
